# Ch.3 RFP Received — The First 24 Hours

> Reading this chapter, you gain the 7 questions to ask the program officer on the day you receive an RFP, plus a checklist for precision-analyzing the RFP itself.

## The First 24 Hours Decide Overall Quality

About 60% of a proposal draft's quality is set on day one. Whatever you miss on the first day cannot be patched by a full week of revision. Two tasks in particular — **(1) the 7 questions for the program officer, and (2) the RFP precision-analysis checklist** — must be finished the same day.

Starting point: when the RFP arrives, print it first, then read it twice closely while marking with three highlighter colors (yellow = scoring criteria, pink = recurring keywords, blue = open questions). Reading only by scrolling on screen causes you to miss table corners and footnotes.

## The 7 Questions to Ask the Program Officer

Every RFP carries a contact line — "for inquiries, reach the issuing agency." Many new PMs never use it, but this is the most honest channel for information outside the official document (see Ch.2 Perspective 5). Program officers do not dislike questions; they dislike formulaic or trivial ones.

The 7 questions are designed to be **"answerable in under five minutes, yet high-value in information."**

1. **What changed this year? What does the funder actually want?**
   - Example: "Compared with last year, where is this year's RFP most different?" / "Could you give the core problem this project tries to solve, in one sentence?"
   - Why it matters: The official wording may read "strengthen self-sustainability," but the real meaning could be "last year's operator walked away mid-project and things broke — this year, make them genuinely self-sustaining."

2. **Has the funder had any prior conversations? What's the atmosphere?**
   - Example: "Were there firms that provided input during the planning phase?" / "Is the evaluation committee similar to last year's?"
   - Why it matters: Surfaces any presumptive-awardee signal and the competitive context. If the officer hedges, treat it as a signal.

3. **Which competitors are expected, and who was last year's operator?**
   - Example: "Is last year's operator likely to rebid? How was their overall performance rated?"
   - Why it matters: First-level input for building a differentiation strategy against competitors.

4. **Is the project actually executable at this budget? What's the reasonable margin?**
   - Example: "Has any prior operator delivered a comparable scope at this budget? What margin do you consider reasonable?"
   - Why it matters: Prevents post-award losses. If the officer says "the budget is tight," check whether scope reduction is open for negotiation.

5. **Which track records must be included, and which can be dropped?**
   - Example: "When listing similar-project track records, which types do you weight most heavily?"
   - Why it matters: Decides which of our assets — the 20,000 entrepreneurs trained, the 10-year history, the 800-coach pool — to put front and center.

6. **Who will be the PM? Do staffing commitments need to be locked?**
   - Example: "At the proposal stage, do you want the PM's real name and CV listed?" / "Is there an approval process for personnel changes?"
   - Why it matters: Even when the RFP does not require 100% dedicated PM allocation, industry practice often expects it.

7. **Any past proposals worth referencing? Which structures worked? What caused losses?**
   - Example: "In last year's winning proposal, was there a section that resonated especially well?" / "Would you be able to share the reason unsuccessful proposals lost?"
   - Why it matters: The fastest way to learn what this funder considers "good."

### Call Handling Tips

- **Do not ask everything at once**: 3–4 questions per call is better. First call, absorb the RFP, then a second, deeper call.
- **Take notes live**: Type while on the phone. Post-call reconstruction loses nuance.
- **Do not quote unofficial content directly in the proposal**: Treat call content as analysis input only. Translate it into your own framing inside the proposal.
- **Create an email record**: After the call, send a "summary of today's discussion" email to capture the important answers on record.

## RFP Precision-Analysis Checklist

After two close readings, organize the following in a table. Excel, Notion, anywhere works, but you must produce a **single-page summary** so it can be shared with the team.

### Basic Information

- [ ] **Project name** (verbatim from the RFP)
- [ ] **Funder / issuing agency** (legal entity · department · program officer contact)
- [ ] **Project purpose** (1–2 core sentences extracted from the RFP)
- [ ] **Scope of work** (each sub-task numbered as a list)
- [ ] **Target beneficiaries** (count · age · region · segment — down to the unit of "30 youth aspiring entrepreneurs")
- [ ] **Scale and budget** (total · whether VAT is included · split disbursement terms)
- [ ] **Schedule** (kickoff · completion · reporting milestones)

### Evaluation Criteria (Top Priority)

- [ ] **Points per scoring item**: e.g., Proposal comprehension 30, Execution Plan 40, Proposer capacity 20, Budget 10
- [ ] **Weighting, bonus points, penalty points** clauses
- [ ] **Quantitative vs. qualitative evaluation** ratio
- [ ] **Evaluation committee composition** (external vs. internal, disciplines)
- [ ] Before drafting, check that your page distribution matches this point distribution

### Format Requirements

- [ ] **Specified table of contents**: If provided, follow it exactly. If absent, design your own in order of scoring weight.
- [ ] **Page limit**: Upper bound on body pages. Whether appendices count toward the limit.
- [ ] **File format**: PDF / HWP / PPT · size limit · filename convention
- [ ] **Submission method**: Online portal / in-person / post · deadline
- [ ] **Required documents**: Business registration, financial statements, performance bond, seals, etc.

### Hidden Clues

- [ ] **Corners of tables**: "+2 bonus points," "required submission," "penalty for non-submission"
- [ ] **Footnotes and remarks columns**: A single line here can set the overall tone
- [ ] **Top 5 recurring keywords**: Words that appear 3+ times in the RFP — use them in the proposal at similar frequency
- [ ] **What's missing from the RFP**: The absent items often hold the key. For example, if no PR plan is mentioned, add one as a Section V Bonus Offering.

### Scope-vs-Budget Sense Check

- [ ] Total budget ÷ number of tasks = average budget per task
- [ ] Estimate staffing capacity (budget × 70% ÷ monthly labor cost = possible headcount × months)
- [ ] "Can this scope actually be delivered at this budget?" Reality check — if not, ask the program officer.

## What You Should Have by End of Day One

Before the day ends, two artifacts must exist.

1. **One-page summary** (the filled-in RFP precision-analysis checklist)
2. **Program-officer call memo** (answers to the 7 questions plus additional nuance)

These two become the inputs for tomorrow morning's team kickoff, where you decide: "Is this worth bidding on?", "What strategy do we take?", "How do we staff the PM and team?"

## First-Day Traps to Avoid

- **Deferring the officer call to "later"** — inquiry windows can be short. Call the same day or the next.
- **Starting on the draft immediately** — drafts written before analysis are almost always thrown away. Structure only appears after analysis.
- **Skipping the scoring-weight breakdown** — if you ignore weights, you fall into Ch.5 Pitfall 4 (sketching the curriculum from the budget only).
- **"I don't know the officer, so I can't call"** — even without rapport, the RFP's contact line is a work line. Do not hesitate.
