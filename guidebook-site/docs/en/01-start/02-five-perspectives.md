# Ch.2 Five Perspectives

> Reading this chapter, you will be able to self-check a proposal draft from five different viewpoints.

## Why "Perspectives"

When a new PM finishes a first draft, two illnesses usually show up. (1) Plenty of bragging, not much of an answer. (2) Plenty of detail, but the RFP has been missed. These illnesses are not about "skill" — they are about **perspective**. Change which lens you are reading through, and most of the problems fix themselves.

The five perspectives below are tools for borrowing the view of the evaluator, the funder's contact person, and the competing bidder. Each comes with three self-check items. Running the 15 items through a first draft surfaces the places that need work.

## 1. Will the Reader Be Convinced

The proposal's readers are the contact who built the RFP and the evaluation committee. Both of them read dozens of pages a day. To convince them, **erase vague expressions, answer in the RFP's keywords, and prove every claim with numbers and cases.**

- **No vague expressions**: "many," "various," "sufficient," "optimal" — these words do not appear without evidence. Instead of "many coaches," write "a nationwide coach pool of 800." Verified figures sit in [Appendix B](../appendix/b-ud-assets.md).
- **Echo the RFP's keywords**: if the RFP uses "self-sustaining capacity" four times, the proposal uses the same word at the same frequency and in the same positions. Do not silently swap it for "sustainability."
- **Numbers and cases, not abstractions**: not "we run this systematically" but "we run this with biweekly offline gatherings + monthly integrated meetings + an always-on KakaoTalk channel."

**Three self-check items:**

- [ ] Does this page still contain vague quantity expressions (`many`, `various`, etc.)? If so, replace with numbers or cut.
- [ ] Are the RFP's top 5 keywords used verbatim in the proposal?
- [ ] Does every claim carry at least one piece of evidence (number, case, tool, or system)?

## 2. Is the Detail Complete

A strong proposal passes this test: **"Could someone else read this proposal alone and execute it?"** Instructor profiles, session-level schedule, budget line items, safety management, even venue circulation — written at a level where the project still runs if the author disappears. That depth of detail is what proves the proposing firm can actually do the work.

- **Instructor and coach profiles**: real names, affiliations, relevant experience, and which session they are assigned to. "10 expert coaches deployed" alone proves nothing.
- **Session-level schedule**: title, goal, deliverable, delivery method, and time breakdown for each session. This resolution lets an evaluator count Action Week share and theory share themselves.
- **Budget line items**: split into personnel, operating, outsourcing, and production costs, with the contents of each line written out. Correct design puts the most budget behind the highest-weighted scoring item.
- **Risk, safety, and reporting**: on-site safety staffing, emergency response manual, monthly / mid-term / final reporting. In B2G, these items carry a larger point share.

**Three self-check items:**

- [ ] Could a colleague unfamiliar with the project read this proposal and produce session-level prep lists and timetables?
- [ ] Are instructors, coaches, and operations staff mapped by real names (or anonymized experience summaries) to specific sessions?
- [ ] Does at least one of safety / risk / reporting get a full paragraph of coverage?

## 3. Did You Actually Read the RFP

**This is the number-one mistake.** The body of the RFP gets read, but tables, footnotes, and margin notes get skipped — and 10 to 20 points slip away. In particular:

- **Scoring rubric**: the point distribution by item is stated in the RFP. Writing 2 pages on a 30-point item and 8 pages on a 10-point item is a reverse allocation.
- **RFP-specified table of contents**: when the RFP says "write the proposal in the following order," never rearrange. Evaluators read with the table of contents in hand.
- **The corner of a table**: phrases like "2 bonus points," "mandatory submission," and "point deduction if not submitted" hide inside tables.
- **Margin notes**: a single line like "this project prioritizes regional economic revitalization" can set the entire tone.
- **Task scope vs. budget ratio**: if there are 5 tasks and the budget is 100 million won, that is 20 million per task. Missing this early sends the curriculum off on its own.

**Three self-check items:**

- [ ] Have the RFP's tables, footnotes, and margin notes been highlighted on a second read?
- [ ] Is there a table of point weights by scoring item, compared against the page distribution in the proposal?
- [ ] If the RFP specifies a table of contents, does the proposal follow it exactly? (Skip if none.)

## 4. Is the Competitive Context Understood

The same RFP goes out to several firms. Evaluators compare proposals side by side. Wins are possible without knowing the competitive context, but the odds improve sharply when it is known.

- **Incumbent likelihood**: is the RFP tailored for a specific firm? Is last year's contractor bidding again? If incumbency is high, the strategy shifts to "what do we bring that flips this?"
- **Last year's feedback**: what disappointed the funder last year. If the feedback was "PR was weak" or "performance metrics were under-managed," we saturate that area in this year's proposal.
- **Differentiation from competitors**: how the main competing firms typically propose. State one or two clear points of difference.
- **Price competitiveness**: rather than chasing the lowest quantity number, narrate "how the same budget produces a larger effect."

**Three self-check items:**

- [ ] Has the contact person or the industry network been used to confirm incumbent or last-year contractor information?
- [ ] Can "the one thing we do clearly differently from competitors" be said in a single sentence?
- [ ] If there is a known disappointment from last year, is there a section that saturates that gap?

## 5. Did You Absorb the Off-Record Information

Information from outside the RFP creates the biggest differentiation. Every firm reads the official document identically — only the firms that move get the unofficial information.

- **Contact-person call**: the call on the day the RFP drops. Seven questions like "what is the change point this year?" and "what matters most to you?" are covered in [Ch.3](../02-field/03-first-24h.md).
- **Site visit**: visiting the target region or institution even once puts lines like "having walked this commercial district in person" into the proposal.
- **Internal knowledge**: the company Slack, past proposal archives, colleagues' experience. Accumulated insight like "this funder looks especially hard at financial stability."
- **Leadership signals**: remarks from the CEO or division head like "we absolutely need to land this one." When that signal lands, resource allocation shifts; when resources shift, quality shifts.

Off-record information **must not be quoted directly**. Putting the contact person's call content into the proposal verbatim puts that person in an awkward position. The information is raw material for analysis — the proposal reinterprets it into a line like "we understand the core purpose of this project to be X."

**Three self-check items:**

- [ ] At least one call with the contact person after the RFP was released?
- [ ] A visit to the target region or institution, or a review of relevant materials?
- [ ] One or two insights drawn from off-record information, reinterpreted and woven into the proposal?

## Five Perspectives on One Page

| Perspective | Core Question | Failure Symptom |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| 1. Persuasiveness | Does it answer with evidence, free of vague expressions? | The evaluator sets it down halfway |
| 2. Detail | Could someone execute from the proposal alone? | "Can this firm actually do this?" |
| 3. RFP reading | Have tables, footnotes, and scoring been read? | Points bleed from high-weight items |
| 4. Competitive context | Is incumbency, competition, and last-year context known? | A "respectable second-place" proposal |
| 5. Off-record information | Are the voices of the contact and the site absorbed? | A proposal anyone could have written |

After finishing a draft, come back to this table, pick the two weakest perspectives, and concentrate time there. Trying to fix all five at once usually fixes none.
