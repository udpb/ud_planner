# Ch.5 Common Pitfalls

> Reading this chapter, you learn the location of 7 landmines that seniors have already stepped on. You should not step on them.

## Pitfall 1. Writing slides before the Value Chain

**Principle**: Confirm the 5-layer causal chain (Input → Activity → Output → Outcome → Impact) on a single page, then open the slides.

### Why this order

Section layout, page allocation, and scoring-item mapping are all derived from the Value Chain. Without it, each section reads as an independent fragment and evaluators must reconstruct "which Activity connects to which Outcome?" on their own. Once reconstruction is required, points are deducted.

### Symptoms when this is violated

- One feedback round flips the section order and logic structure
- Page count grows, but "So what?" has no answer
- The same Activity is narrated differently by different team members
- Structural revisions continue until the deadline, leaving typos and broken sentences

### Response — in this order

```
1. RFP precision analysis (Ch.3)
       │
       ▼
2. Confirm 5-layer Value Chain on one page (Ch.4)
       │     ← No slide work before this point
       ▼
3. Team sharing · division-head approval
       │
       ▼
4. Section-by-section slide writing (Ch.6)
```

### Checkpoints

- [ ] Are all 5 layers on a single A4 page?
- [ ] Does the "So what?" test pass between every pair of layers?
- [ ] Has the entire team (including the division head) reviewed the same page together?
- [ ] Did slide work start only after that agreement?

> **Reference case**: Kolon Pro Bono — 2 weeks of infinite revisions writing slides without a Value Chain. After a mid-stream reset, a half-day Value Chain confirmation led to completion in 1.5 days, with improved quality. (See Korean edition casebook.)

## Pitfall 2. Listing theory, missing field-execution components

**Principle**: Regardless of business type, the proposal must reveal a **"segment where the beneficiary actually does something."** If theory and lectures are stacked, the proposal reads as weak in execution.

### Why this principle is business-type-agnostic

Evaluators consistently ask: **"When this project ends, what actually remains for the beneficiary?"** What remains is not a theory summary — it is **experience, deliverables, and data gathered in the field**. The form of "doing" varies by business type, but the principle is the same.

### Forms of "doing" by business type

| Business Type | Form of the Execution Segment |
|---|---|
| Startup training | Action Week — prototype testing, field sales, customer interviews |
| Small-business support | Store-renovation field application, actual-sales testing, customer-response collection |
| Local commerce | Pop-up operation, branding showcase, in-district collaboration |
| Design competitions | Physical sample production, retail-entry trials, public-response gathering |
| Culture & craft | Joint-creation sessions, on-site demonstration, exchange participation |
| Matching (mentoring / pro bono) | Joint mentor-beneficiary projects, mid-course check, final presentation |
| Global expansion | Overseas on-site visits, buyer meetings, MVP in-market testing |

### Symptoms when this is violated

- Three consecutive theory sessions → participant attention drops; evaluators read "weak execution"
- Curriculum has 50%+ "lecture / special talk" and less than 10% "application / execution"
- Deliverables stop at "certificates / reports" and fail to connect to Outcome numbers

### Response

- When 3 consecutive theory sessions appear, insert at least one **"doing" segment** suited to the business type
- Predefine a 3-category ratio in the curriculum table: **theory / application / execution** (recommended: theory 30–40%, application + execution 60–70%)
- Connect execution-segment deliverables to Output and Outcome

### Checkpoints

- [ ] Is there no stretch of 3 consecutive theory sessions in the curriculum?
- [ ] Is a "doing" segment matched to the business type explicitly marked?
- [ ] Do the execution-segment deliverables connect to scoring items?

> Reference: Underdogs' execution philosophy — **"You know nothing until you've done it."** (See Appendix B.)

## Pitfall 3. Vague quantity expressions

"Many experts," "various programs," "sufficient support," "optimal environment" — these signal to evaluators that **"we have no data to back our quantities."** They violate the quantitative-saturation principle.

### Replacement examples

| Vague expression | Quantitatively saturated expression |
|---|---|
| Many coaches | Nationwide 800 coach pool, 261 action coaches (verify case-specific figures) |
| Various programs | 5 programs per week, total 40 sessions over 8 weeks |
| Sufficient support | 1:1 coaching weekly, expert mentor monthly, peer network always-on |
| Abundant network | 520 global partners, 93 municipal-level project histories |

**Response**: After the draft is complete, Ctrl+F the five words: `many`, `various`, `sufficient`, `abundant`, `optimal`. Replace every occurrence with a number, or remove the sentence.

## Pitfall 4. Sketching the curriculum from the budget only

Every RFP has a scoring rubric. If you ignore the weight distribution and "match the curriculum roughly to the budget," you end up allocating few pages to the highest-weighted item.

### Typical reverse allocation (failure)

- Scoring weights: Execution plan 40, Expected effects 30, Proposer capacity 20, Budget 10
- Proposal: Company introduction 8pp, Execution plan 5pp, Expected effects 2pp, Budget 1pp
- **Result**: The heaviest-weighted item (Execution plan) is the thinnest section → loss of 20–30 points

### Correct distribution (success)

- Same weights as above
- Execution plan 14pp, Expected effects 8pp, Company introduction 4pp, Budget 1pp + Section V bonus 1pp

**Response**: Before drafting, build a scoring-weight table and roughly calculate "page count per item." After drafting, compare actual distribution with target distribution.

## Pitfall 5. Briefing researchers without direction

When juniors, interns, or external researchers are brought in to support writing, briefs like "please research competing projects" produce useless output or require rework.

### Bad vs. good research briefs

- **Bad**: "Please research similar-project cases."
- **Good**: "Please find 3 local-brand projects from 2023–2025 in Gyeonggi-do, Seoul, or Busan, with budgets ≥ $300K and durations ≥ 6 months. For each, summarize on one A4 page: (1) key message, (2) 5-layer Value Chain, (3) differentiation points. Cite sources as press releases or result reports."

**Difference**: Specifying scope, criteria, format, and source lets the researcher act immediately. Without direction, researchers guess and end up reworking.

**Response**: Before briefing a researcher, spend 10 minutes writing a brief that explicitly covers **scope, criteria, format, source**. Check in at the first third of the work.

## Pitfall 6. Missing measurement and reporting structure

**Principle**: For every business type, the proposal must reveal **"what is measured, how, and reported to whom."** Weak measurement/reporting severs evaluation, renewal, and follow-on business.

### Why measurement/reporting is business-type-agnostic

Each funder has a different **destination** for the performance report. The destination differs; the report itself is a universal requirement.

| Funder Type | Report Destination |
|---|---|
| B2G (central / local / public) | Government performance evaluation · audit · national-assembly audit · next-year budget framing |
| B2B (large / mid-size enterprises) | Sustainability report · ESG report · executive briefing |
| Local government | Local council · public hearing · regional economic indicators |
| Foundations / associations | Board · donors · corporate sponsor periodic reports |
| Overseas projects | Overseas partners · buyers · headquarters English KPIs |

### Measurement targets by business type

| Business Type | Primary Metrics |
|---|---|
| Startup training | Pre/post capacity diagnostics · startup rate · revenue occurrence rate · follow-on-support linkage |
| Small-business support | Store-revenue change · ticket size · revisit rate · online conversion |
| Local commerce | Empty-storefront occupancy · district foot traffic · merchant-association activity |
| Competitions | Winning-entry retail-entry rate · sales performance · export count |
| Culture & craft | Joint-creation count · international-exchange persistence · partner-country count |
| Matching / pro bono | Matching satisfaction · beneficiary-org KPI change · re-participation rate |
| Global expansion | Overseas entities · overseas revenue · overseas investment raised · buyer contracts |

### Response structure

- Place a **"Performance Measurement & Reporting"** section of 1–2 pages in every proposal
- Use a 4-column table: **metric · measurement tool · timing · report cadence**
- When applicable, use Underdogs' diagnostic tools (ACT-PRENEURSHIP, DOGS, 6 Dimension) as quantification backing
- Beyond mid-term and final reports, propose a **follow-up report** 3–6 months after project end

### Checkpoints

- [ ] Are measurement targets set to match the business type?
- [ ] Are measurement timings (pre / mid / post / trailing) specified?
- [ ] Are the **recipient · format · cadence** of the report all concrete?
- [ ] Is the format usable for downstream needs (renewal / next project / audit)?

## Pitfall 7. Missing Section V (out-of-RFP-scope proposals)

Proposals that fill only the RFP's required scope become "respectable second-place" bids. Winning proposals consistently add **Section V Bonus Offerings** at the end — 3–4 items outside the RFP scope.

### Section V usual items

- **Global linkage**: Japan and India entity leverage, overseas expo participation, global buyer matching
- **Impact report**: Subsidiary Korea Social Value Assessment, ESG-value measurement methodology
- **Follow-on investment**: LICORN investment review, matching with KOSME LIPS financing (up to 5×)
- **Post-program community**: Alumni network, peer-mentoring connections
- **Additional content**: Video archiving, external-collaboration exhibitions, competition linkage

Each item must sit at the **intersection** of "something only we can do" and "something that serves the RFP's purpose." Irrelevant items in Section V backfire as "weak task understanding."

**Response**: Before finishing the draft, brainstorm 3–4 "things not requested by the RFP that we can provide." Add a one-sentence explanation of how each connects to the RFP's core purpose.

## 7 Pitfalls Summary

| # | Pitfall | Core Response |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Slides before Value Chain | Value Chain on one page first (Ch.4) |
| 2 | Theory listing, missing execution | ≥1 "doing" segment fit to business type |
| 3 | Vague quantities | Replace `many`, `various` with numbers or specific nouns |
| 4 | Budget-first curriculum | Page allocation based on scoring weights |
| 5 | Direction-less research briefs | Specify scope · criteria · format · source |
| 6 | Missing measurement/reporting | 4-column table: metric · tool · timing · cadence |
| 7 | Missing Section V | 3–4 out-of-RFP-scope offerings |

## Escaping the Pitfalls

Rather than memorizing the pitfalls, the habit of **running the checklist** is more effective. Appendix A (Pre-submission Checklist — currently Korean only) reflects all 7. Run it once after the first draft and once before submission, and most pitfalls are filtered.
