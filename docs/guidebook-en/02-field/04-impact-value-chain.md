# Ch.4 Impact Value Chain Thinking

> Reading this chapter, you will learn to distinguish the five layers — Input → Activity → Output → Outcome → Impact — and use the "So what?" test to check the causal chain.

## Why Value Chain

The most common failure mode in proposals is a **broken causal chain** between "what we will do" and "the impact we will create." For example:

> Train 30 entrepreneurs for 8 weeks → Regional economic revitalization

There is no intermediate step linking "train 30 people" to "economic revitalization." Neither evaluators, funders, nor we ourselves can be persuaded. This break is not just "rough writing" — it is a **structural defect that collapses the fundamental persuasiveness of the proposal**.

The Impact Value Chain is the simplest tool to prevent this break. We divide the path from activity to impact into five layers, and throw "So what?" at each boundary to verify that the causality makes sense.

## 5 Layers Defined

| Layer | Definition | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Input** | Resources invested — money, people, time, space, network | Budget $5M, 2 PMs, 10 coaches, 5 classrooms, 3 regional partner organizations |
| **Activity** | Actions performed — what we actually did | 8-week startup training, 40 coaching sessions, 1 Demo Day |
| **Output** | Immediate deliverables — direct results of activities (quantifiable) | 28 graduates, 28 business plans, 15 MVP products |
| **Outcome** | Changed state — short-to-medium-term change in participants or field | (by business type) Startup: 40% startup rate, avg. monthly revenue $2K / Small business: 30% revenue increase, 20% ticket size increase / Local commerce: 15% reduction in empty storefronts, 40% revisit rate / Design competition: 3 winning entries entering retail, 2 exports / Culture & craft: 2 international joint exhibitions, regularized partner-country exchange |
| **Impact** | Long-term social value — system- or ecosystem-level change that persists after the project ends | Reduced regional youth outflow, 20% increase in local alley-commerce revenue, 1 self-sustaining merchant association formed |

The two distinctions to master:

### Output vs. Outcome

The most commonly confused boundary.

- **Output** = "what came out" (countable right after the event ends)
- **Outcome** = "what changed" (actual change in participants or field)

Examples — by business type:

| Situation (Business Type) | Output | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| Startup training, 8 weeks | 30 graduates, 30 business plans, 15 MVPs | Readiness up 70%, 12 actual startups, avg. monthly revenue $2K |
| Small-business growth support, 6 months | 25 renovated stores, 25 operations-training graduates | 30% avg. revenue increase, 20% ticket-size increase, 40% revisit rate |
| Local commerce brand week | 10,000 visitors, 40 participating stores, 5 pop-ups | 30% district revenue increase, 3 empty storefronts re-occupied, 40% revisit rate |
| Competition (design/souvenir) | 600 entries, 30 winning entries | 5 winning entries entering retail, cumulative revenue $200K, 2 exports |
| Culture & craft exchange | 15 joint-creation works, 3 international events | 2 international joint exhibitions, 5 overseas orders for participating artisans, exchange platform established |
| Social impact / matching | 30 matched pairs, 30 joint projects | Beneficiary-org KPI up 2.3× on average, 80% employee re-participation intent |
| Global expansion | 3 on-site visits, 40 buyer meetings | 3 overseas entities established, 5 teams generating overseas revenue, 2 teams receiving overseas investment |

Showing only Output stops at "we did it." Adding Outcome elevates it to "we were effective." Winning proposals **pair Output and Outcome** as two-line sets.

### Activity vs. Output

Also frequently confused.

- **Activity** = verb ("we do ~", "we conduct ~")
- **Output** = noun/quantity ("30 graduates", "30 business plans")

"We conduct 8 weeks of coaching" is Activity. "40 coaching sessions × 30 participants = 1,200 1:1 coaching instances" is Output.

## The "So What?" Test

The easiest way to check whether causality is broken between layers is to **say "So what?" out loud**. Example:

> Activity: Run an 8-week startup training program
> **So what?**
> Output: 30 graduates, 15 MVPs
> **So what?**
> Outcome: 12 startups formed, avg. monthly revenue $2K
> **So what?**
> Impact: Regional startup culture spreads; voluntary applicants for the next cohort double

If each "So what?" has an answer, the causality is connected. If no answer comes, a layer is missing. Most breaks occur **between Output and Outcome**. "30 graduates" is easy to write, but "what changed for participants" requires data.

### Typical Break Patterns

1. **Activity → Impact jump**: Skipping intermediate layers, like "training → regional revitalization." The most common.
2. **Output/Outcome confusion**: Treating graduate count as Outcome. Graduation is a result of participation, not a change.
3. **Abstract Outcomes**: Unmeasurable expressions like "entrepreneurial mindset improvement." Replace with **numbers fitted to the business type**:
   - Startup training: "Pre/post ACT-PRENEURSHIP score 35 → 52"
   - Small business: "Avg. store monthly revenue $2K → $2.6K (30% increase)"
   - Local commerce: "Empty storefronts 12 → 9 (25% reduction), revisit rate 35%"
   - Design competition: "3 of 10 winning entries enter retail, cumulative revenue $150K"
   - Culture & craft: "5 international joint creations, regularized 3×/year exchange with partner country"
   - Matching / pro bono: "Beneficiary-org KPI up 2.3× on avg., 80% employee re-participation intent"
4. **Impact belongs to someone else**: Metrics so broad that our project's direct contribution is hard to claim. "National GDP growth" is not Impact.

## Applying in Practice (abstract example)

One abstract practice. Concrete examples are plentiful in Part 3 Casebook.

Hypothetical RFP: "Gyeonggi-do small-business branding support, budget $300K, target 20 teams, 6 months."

5-layer draft:

- **Input**: Budget $300K, 1 PM, 5 dedicated coaches, 10-person branding expert advisory panel, 1 LMS
- **Activity**: Pre-diagnosis (ACT-PRENEURSHIP) → 6-week branding workshop → 2-week Action Week (field application) → 120 1:1 coaching sessions → integrated showcase
- **Output**: 18 graduating teams, 18 brand guides, 18 prototypes, 2,000 showcase visitors
- **Outcome**: Avg. monthly revenue from $1.5K → $2.4K (60% increase), SNS followers up 3× on average, 3 teams receiving follow-up investment/loans
- **Impact**: Gyeonggi-do small-business branding casebook published, benchmarking inquiries from other localities, self-forming branding group inside the regional chamber of commerce

Running "So what?" through this draft, most layers connect. If Outcome figures lack backing, that is a weakness — support them with measured data from past projects or label the source ("based on past similar-project averages").

## Why Learn This Thinking Method

First, **persuasiveness**. A clear Value Chain lets evaluators map each layer onto scoring items. If the scoring item is "Expected Effects," concentrate on Outcome + Impact. If it is "Execution Plan," write Activity + Output densely.

Second, **team alignment**. When a new PM writes alone, the draft tends to become Activity-heavy. When the division head, CM, and finance lead read a single Value Chain page together, decisions such as "Outcome is weak — what data should we add?" become tractable.

Third, **reduced revision cost**. As discussed in Ch.5 Pitfall 1 (with the Kolon Pro Bono reference case), writing slides before a Value Chain means every feedback round flips the whole. With a confirmed Value Chain, only section-level local edits are needed.

## Review Checklist

After finishing a draft:

- [ ] Are each of the 5 layers written explicitly on one line?
- [ ] When "So what?" is inserted between Activity → Output → Outcome → Impact, is there an answer?
- [ ] Are Output and Outcome not mixed up?
- [ ] Is Outcome backed by numbers, diagnostic scores, or evidence?
- [ ] Is Impact within the realistic contribution scope of our project?
- [ ] Does each layer map to a scoring item?
