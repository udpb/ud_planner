# Ch.1 Why Read This

> Reading this chapter, you will walk away with a one-line answer to the question: "What actually makes a winning proposal different?"

## One-Line Summary

A proposal that wins does not say "we are good." It says **"we understand exactly what the funder needs, we answer in the funder's language, and we back every claim with evidence."** This guidebook organizes the thinking frames a new PM uses to build that "exact understanding" and "evidence-based answer."

## The "We Are Good" Trap

The most common mistake a new PM makes on a first proposal is stacking up company bragging and past accomplishments. Cumulative 50 billion won, 800 coaches, 20,000 entrepreneurs trained — numbers like this are undeniably strong, but they belong **distributed across the body of the proposal in a quantitative-saturation pattern**, not dumped onto a single company-profile page that evaluators will skim past. What evaluators actually want to know is whether the proposing firm **read the RFP correctly**.

In practice, the line between winning and losing a bid almost always comes down to the same questions:

- Does the table of contents follow the RFP's evaluation criteria directly?
- Do the keywords that recur in the RFP appear in the proposal at a similar frequency and tone?
- Has the "one thing" the funder wants to change this year been identified?
- Do numbers, cases, and citations replace abstract expressions like "many" or "various"?

None of these four depend on company size. A first-year PM can do them in week one; a ten-year veteran can still miss them in a rush.

## Answer in the Funder's Language

The funder's language lives in two places.

1. **The RFP body, tables, footnotes, and margin notes** — the official language. An expression tucked into the corner of a table like "bonus points awarded at evaluation" can swing 20 points.
2. **Channels outside the official document** — the nuance of a phone call with the contact person, the real question behind a Slack or email thread, the unresolved feedback from last year's contractor, the passing remark from leadership that "this one matters this year."

Reading only the official language produces a proposal that looks like every other submission. Absorbing the unofficial language too creates the signal "this firm actually understands us." Ask the contact person and they will answer around 70% of the time — stay silent and the answer is 0%. [Ch.3 The First 24 Hours After an RFP](../02-field/03-first-24h.md) covers how to ask.

## Answer with Evidence

Anyone can write "we are good at training young entrepreneurs." Evidence stacks in four layers.

- **Quantitative evidence**: numbers. Cumulative figures like "20,000+ entrepreneurs trained," "10 years of operation," "93 regions" (precise values in [Appendix B](../appendix/b-ud-assets.md))
- **Case evidence**: wins and executions with a similar structure. "The NH Agritech competition was designed with this same approach."
- **Tool evidence**: proprietary diagnostics and methodology. ACT-PRENEURSHIP, DOGS, 6 Dimension Startup Growth Model, IMPACT Startup Methodology
- **System evidence**: the 4-Tier Support System (expert mentor pool · consultant pool · dedicated coach · peer network)

The goal is not to use all four layers. The goal is to **place one or two of the most appropriate layers against each question the RFP asks**. Theory-centered proposals lean on tool and system evidence; performance-centered proposals lean on quantitative and case evidence.

## The One Sentence to Internalize First

> **Start with "what the funder needs," not "what we are good at."**

When this order flips, the proposal becomes a generic marketing piece. When the order holds, evaluators read all the way to the end.
