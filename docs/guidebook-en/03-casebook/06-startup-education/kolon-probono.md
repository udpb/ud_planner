# Kolon Pro Bono — Value Chain Design Lesson Case

> Reading this case, you learn **how a PM who wrote slides without a Value Chain wasted two weeks, and how the direction was locked down in half a day**. The original source of the guidebook's most-emphasized lesson.

## 1. Business One-Line Summary

- **Funder**: Kolon (large-enterprise B2B — pro bono / employee-talent-donation program)
- **Program Name**: Kolon Pro Bono Program operation (a CSR education/event program in which employees help social enterprises and startups)
- **Target**: Kolon Group employees + external social-sector founders and organizations
- **Core Tasks**: Match employees with founders and run talent-donation, mentoring, and collaboration projects
- **Duration**: Annual / half-year cycles

This case is more famous for the **lesson it delivered to internal PM workflow** than for the contract-award outcome itself. It is the real-world reference for Pitfall 1 in Ch.5 of the guidebook.

## 2. Key Messages

Summary of the original tone:

> **"임직원의 전문성을 사회 문제 해결과 연결하는 프로보노 매칭 + 실행 중심 멘토링 플랫폼"**
> ("A pro bono matching + execution-centered mentoring platform that links employee expertise to social-problem solving.")

Given the B2B pro bono character, both axes — "employee participation satisfaction" and "social impact" — must be captured. Satisfaction high but impact weak reads as "it ended as an event"; impact strong but participation experience weak reads as "low re-participation rate." Both are scored.

## 3. Impact Value Chain — 5 Layers

- **Input**: Kolon employee mentor pool · Underdogs dedicated PM · social-sector founder / organization DB · matching platform · proprietary methodologies (IMPACT, ACT-PRENEURSHIP) · 4-Tier Support System
- **Activity**: Employee mentor recruitment and diagnosis → collecting founder / organization needs → matching → OT + project kickoff → weekly mentoring (fixed sessions) → Action Week (field application) → results presentation and performance sharing
- **Output**: Number of matched pairs, completed projects, employee participation hours, founder deliverables
- **Outcome**: Employee participation satisfaction, capacity change in founders and organizations (diagnostic-based), actual business-progress change, re-participation intent
- **Impact**: Pro bono program established within Kolon's CSR portfolio, expansion of the social-sector network, accumulated social-value solidarity among employees

## 4. Three Differentiation Points

1. **Match-quality management** — Employees and founders are not matched randomly but **paired on the basis of pre-diagnosis**. Employees' areas of expertise and founders' needs are aligned via the ACT-PRENEURSHIP 5-capacity matrix.
2. **Action Week inserted** — Prevents three consecutive theory-mentoring sessions by inserting a mid-program field-execution week that verifies "has anything really changed?" Keeps employee engagement up and gives founders real application experience.
3. **Performance-data reporting** — Rather than simple participation photos, the report bundles participants' pre/post diagnostic scores, founders' KPI change, and qualitative interviews into a **format directly usable in Kolon's sustainability report**.

## 5. How Underdogs' Proprietary Tools and Network Worked

- **ACT-PRENEURSHIP**: Used both for employee-mentor pre-diagnosis (mapping expertise) and for founder pre/post diagnosis.
- **IMPACT Startup Methodology**: The default frame for founder coaching. Of the 6 stages, only the modules matching the participating founder's current stage are selected.
- **DOGS Team Building**: Since the matched pair must move as one team, deployed once early on (DISC-based 24 questions).
- **4-Tier Support System**: Kolon employee mentors (specialist mentor panel) + external consultant pool + Underdogs dedicated coach + peers (other matched pairs) network. Avoids a single-mentor structure.
- **ESG measurement know-how across 1,600 companies**: Applied to the reporting format linked to Kolon's sustainability report.

## 6. Lessons for New PMs

The **real lesson** of this case lies in the writing process more than in the program content.

**(1) The lesson of two weeks spent writing slides without a Value Chain.** Early on, the PM received the RFP and immediately started filling in PPT sections. Each section was packed with "showcasing our capability + listing tasks," but every round of feedback from the CEO and the division head flipped the logical structure, so the section order kept getting reshuffled. **Two weeks of infinite revision**. Even when a final version emerged, "what impact this pro bono creates" never condensed into a single line.

**(2) The effect of the half-day reset.** Mid-stream, the whole team stopped and drew the 5 layers (Input → Activity → Output → Outcome → Impact) on a single A4 page. After that:
- Filled each layer with key numbers, tools, and evidence (half a day)
- Shared this one page across the team (common language secured)
- Section-by-section writing finished in **1.5 days**
- Feedback converged to local edits at the level of "reinforce this Outcome figure's basis"
- Final-version quality was visibly better than the prior two-week version

**(3) The lesson in numbers.** Two weeks of infinite revision → half-day Value Chain + 1.5 days of writing = about 2 days. This is not "8× faster" — rather, the content produced over those two weeks never became usable material, so **effectively the entire time was saved**.

**(4) Embedding the lesson as process.** Two habits internalize this lesson:
- **Day 1–2 after the RFP**: Call with the funder contact + RFP precision analysis + one-page 5-layer Value Chain ([Ch.3](../../02-field/03-first-24h.md), [Ch.4](../../02-field/04-impact-value-chain.md))
- **Before starting the draft, share with the team**: The CEO, division head, PM, and supporters look at the one Value Chain page together and agree "does this make sense?" Only after that agreement does section-writing begin.

**(5) The particularity of pro bono programs.** Unlike general startup education, one axis is **experience design for B2B-corporate employees**. To make employees feel "this was meaningful":
- A report that shows impact-per-hour-participated in concrete numbers
- Evidence that the employees' own expertise actually contributed
- Storytelling materials usable in an internal CSR presentation

Putting these three into Section V makes the score gap in B2B corporate evaluation.

**(6) Caution.** "Pro bono" has high external-PR value, which makes it easy for corporations to let it end as an **event**. As the proposer, guard against the event frame and design a cycle of **measurement → improvement → re-participation**. Only then does next year's renewal / 2nd cohort follow.
