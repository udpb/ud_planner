## Slide 2 — Why This Matters / Workshop Goal
**Duration**: 2 min

**Speaking notes**:

> The one-night, two-day hackathon is not a shorter version of a three-month batch. It is a different instrument with a different job. A batch changes a founder's trajectory over months. A hackathon changes a participant's self-image over 36 hours. We use it for three jobs: to filter — finding the teams worth investing batch resources in; to activate — turning a passive learner into someone who has shipped something; and to localize — stress-testing our planning playbook against a specific country, a specific city, a specific problem pool. By the end of today, you should be able to design and run a 1N2D that reflects Underdogs' philosophy, localizes to your country context, and connects pre-event LMS preparation with post-event follow-up.

## Slide 5 — The Planning Philosophy (6 Principles)
**Duration**: 3 min

**Speaking notes**:

> Six principles guide every program we design. One — **data flows top-down — each step receives the previous step's output as its input**. The RFP shapes the curriculum; the curriculum shapes the coach selection; the coaches shape the budget. We never re-type the same input twice. Two — internal assets rise automatically. When you land on the curriculum step, our 18 IMPACT modules, 800 coaches, and SROI proxy library surface without you searching for them. Three — AI is called inside context, not from scratch. Every Claude or Gemini call we make carries the accumulated pipeline context with it. Four — a new planner understands not just what to do but why. Every step ships with an embedded guide, reference, and warning. Five — Impact-First is rebuilt on top of the curriculum. We auto-extract Activities from curriculum sessions, then let AI generate Outcome and Impact. Six — Action Week is mandatory. Three consecutive theory sessions triggers a warning; we insert an Action Week block.

**Transition**: "The backbone that carries all six is one diagram — the Impact Value Chain."

## Slide 6 — Impact Value Chain (5 Layers)
**Duration**: 3 min

**Speaking notes**:

> Imagine a coach receives an RFP for a six-month small-business program in Gyeonggi-do. The RFP lists a budget and a target audience — that is Input. A good planner writes a session calendar — that is Activity. The session produces a pitch deck and a tested prototype — that is Output. Two months later, participants report they landed their first paying customer — that is Outcome. A year later, the local district measures a 3% uptick in the small-business survival rate — that is Impact. Five layers: Input, Activity, Output, Outcome, Impact. I, A, O, Oc, Im. Every proposal we write flows through all five. If a layer is missing, the proposal is incomplete — and trained evaluators can spot the missing layer in under 30 seconds.

**Transition**: "And evaluators spot it using one question."

---

## Slide 7 — The "So What?" Test
**Duration**: 2 min

**Speaking notes**:

> The So What test is the single most useful diagnostic in our playbook. Walk up the value chain from Input. "We secured a 500-million-won budget." So what? "We ran 20 sessions." So what? "We produced 15 team prototypes." So what? "Five teams landed paying customers." So what? "The district's small-business survival rate moved three points." At each So What, if the next layer is thin or missing, you know exactly where the proposal will lose points. We train our planners to apply this test four times before submitting anything. It is free. It takes 90 seconds. It catches 70% of weak proposals.

**Transition**: "But the value chain does not tell you which methodology to use. That is a separate decision."

## Slide 8 — Methodology is NOT One-Size — 9 Primary Methodologies
**Duration**: 3 min

**Speaking notes**:

> Underdogs runs nine primary methodologies. IMPACT — our 18-module social-enterprise curriculum. 로컬 — local regeneration. 글로컬 — glocal, for teams straddling local and global markets. 공모전 — competition-style. 매칭 — founder-to-expert matching. 재창업 — re-founding for founders pivoting after a failure. 글로벌진출 — global market entry. 소상공인 — small-business growth, the most common in Korean public-sector RFPs. And 커스텀 — custom, the fallback for briefs that fit none of the above. **Not every project is IMPACT. The methodology choice is the first strategic decision.** Get it wrong on Monday, and by Friday your curriculum, coach pool, and budget are all misaligned.

**Transition**: "Underneath every methodology we measure the same five competencies."

## Slide 9 — 5D Competencies (ACTT Extension)
**Duration**: 3 min

**Speaking notes**:

> The 5D framework is how we measure what changes inside a participant. D1 Desire — why do you want this? We look for a clear personal stake. D2 Direction — where are you going? A specific goal, a near-term milestone. D3 Discipline — how do you execute? Habit, cadence, self-monitoring. D4 Design — how do you prototype? Can you test an assumption with an MVP? D5 Durability — how do you sustain? Resilience, a learning loop, a team. These map cleanly onto the Impact Value Chain. Desire and Direction feed the Outcome and Impact layers — the why. Discipline and Design feed the Activity and Output layers — the how. Durability alone feeds long-term Impact. When we assess a team after a 1N2D, we score them on the 5D axes, not on polish.

**Transition**: "Methodology and 5D are the what. The next layer is the how of support."

---

## Slide 10 — 4-Tier Support (Where It Applies; Where It Doesn't)
**Duration**: 2 min

**Speaking notes**:

> Underdogs operates a 4-Tier support structure: Tier 1, foundational curriculum; Tier 2, coach-led cohort; Tier 3, 1-on-1 expert matching; Tier 4, post-program alumni network. In a three-month batch, all four tiers run in parallel. In a 1N2D, we compress aggressively. Tier 1 moves to pre-event LMS — 2 to 3 weeks of async video. Tier 2 runs live during the hackathon. Tier 3, 1-on-1 coaching, is the most expensive tier per hour, so we ration it to three or four sessions per team during the 36 hours. Tier 4 begins the Monday after the hackathon closes. A common mistake is trying to cram all four tiers into the hackathon weekend itself. Don't. The hackathon is Tier 2 with a dense injection of Tier 3.

**Transition**: "And whatever tier you are running, one principle is non-negotiable."

---

## Slide 11 — Execution-Guaranteed: Action Week Principle
**Duration**: 2 min

**Speaking notes**:

> Action Week is our non-negotiable rule. After three consecutive theory sessions, the next block must be hands-on. Our planning system enforces this — it will literally warn you when you stack four lectures in a row. In a 1N2D, the ratio inverts: theory is compressed to the pre-event LMS, and the on-site 36 hours are almost entirely execution. Every 2-hour block on-site has to end with a visible, tangible deliverable — a worksheet completed, a customer interview run, a prototype screen built, a pitch line drafted. If a block ends with "we discussed," the block has failed.

**Transition**: "This discipline is supported by one more habit — saturating everything with numbers."

---

## Slide 12 — Quantitative Saturation + Section V Bonus
**Duration**: 2 min

**Speaking notes**:

> Quantitative saturation is a writing discipline we stole from the proposal-winning patterns of our top PMs. Every slide, every line item, every coach assignment carries a number: 3 sessions per team, 800 coaches in the pool, 22% no-show target, 18 IMPACT modules, 520+ partners. Numbers bypass argument. When an evaluator reads "we provide intensive 1-on-1 coaching," they shrug. When they read "3-to-4 coaching sessions per team, 20 to 30 minutes each, 12 coaches per 40-participant cohort," they circle it. Section V Bonus refers to the optional value-add section where winning proposals stack extra numeric commitments — alumni touchpoints, follow-up surveys, Stage 2 referral rates.

## Slide 14 — Short-Format vs Long-Format Tradeoffs
**Duration**: 3 min

**Speaking notes**:

> A three-month batch installs all five D-competencies — Desire, Direction, Discipline, Design, Durability — but costs a participant roughly 200 hours of opportunity cost and costs the operator around 15 million won per participant all-in. A 1N2D installs one, maybe two D-competencies well, costs a participant 36 hours, and costs the operator roughly 600,000 won per participant. Different instruments, different jobs. A 1N2D is worse than a batch at producing founders. It is better than a batch at screening candidates, activating identity, and stress-testing a local problem pool. We design 1N2Ds as the top of a funnel — 20,000 reachable → 1,000 registered → 100 attending the hackathon → 10 finalists graduating into the batch.

**Transition**: "That funnel is the organizing image of the format."

---

## Slide 15 — The Funnel: Pre-event LMS → 1N2D → Follow-up Batch
**Duration**: 3 min

**Speaking notes**:

> The funnel has three stages. Stage 1, pre-event: 2 to 3 weeks of async LMS video, 5 to 8 videos at 10 to 15 minutes each, plus a reading kit of 3 to 5 short pieces, plus one pre-event individual exercise. Stage 2, the 1N2D itself: 36 hours in person, 8 blocks, 3 coaching touches per team. Stage 3, follow-up: a 1-pager written report per team within two weeks, optional Stage-2 batch onboarding for the top teams, quarterly alumni check-in. This is the same skeleton we use across all five country tracks. The localization happens inside the blocks, not to the skeleton.

**Transition**: "Inside those 36 hours, attention is the scarce resource."

---

## Slide 16 — Engagement Techniques for 24 Hours
**Duration**: 3 min

**Speaking notes**:

> Seven engagement techniques. One — milestone compression: each 2-hour block produces a visible deliverable, not a conversation. Two — 1:1 coaching density: 3 to 4 sessions per team, 20 to 30 minutes each. Three — peer accountability: a 2-minute public standup from each team, twice a day. Four — immersive environment: shared dinner, optional overnight on site. Five — physical artifact: every team leaves with a tangible output — a poster, a board, a live URL. Six — public demo: external judges on Day 2, not just internal coaches. Seven — celebration: a formal award ceremony, a group photo, a post-event social. The mistake teams make is thinking engagement comes from motivational speeches. It does not. It comes from structure — from a clock the participants can see, and a deliverable they cannot fake.

**Transition**: "Here is the structural backbone we use."

---

## Slide 17 — The 8-Block 1N2D Template
**Duration**: 3 min

**Speaking notes**:

> Eight blocks. Day 1 morning, block 1: opening and icebreaker, 9 to 10. Block 2: problem deep-dive, track-specific, 10 to 12. Lunch. Day 1 afternoon, block 3: team formation plus ideation, 1 to 3. Block 4: MVP sprint one with the first round of 1-on-1 coaching, 3 to 6. Dinner. Day 1 evening, block 5: evening sprint, social bonding, informal coaching, 7 to 10. Day 2 morning, block 6: MVP sprint two, polish and demo prep, 8 to 10. Block 7: dry-run with coach feedback, 10 to 12. Lunch. Day 2 afternoon, block 8: the demo day proper, 5-minute pitch plus Q&A, then judging, awards, and a 1-hour next-steps briefing. Eight blocks. This is the template. Every country track in Part D is a localization of this template, not a replacement of it.

**Transition**: "The connective tissue between the blocks is the coaching cadence."

---

## Slide 18 — Coaching Cadence (3-4 1:1 Sessions per Team)
**Duration**: 2 min

**Speaking notes**:

> Every team gets 3 to 4 coaching sessions, 20 to 30 minutes each. Session 1 is problem validation — is this the right problem? Session 2 is business-model sanity — who pays, how much, via what channel? Session 3 is pitch polish — what is the single sentence that lands? An optional session 4 is impact framing — how does this connect to the Outcome layer? We map coaches to teams at the end of Day 1 block 1, after teams are formed. A coach typically carries two to three teams over the 36 hours. This cadence is borrowed directly from the SU Team EASI LEAGUE pattern — they proved at scale that 1-on-1 density is the single biggest lever on participant outcome.

**Transition**: "And at the end, the whole thing gets measured on four axes."

---

## Slide 19 — Assessment Rubric (4 Axes)
**Duration**: 2 min

**Speaking notes**:

> Four axes, 25% each. Idea specificity: is the problem concrete, is the insight non-obvious? Business-model completeness: who pays, how much, via what channel, are the unit economics defensible? Growth potential: can this 10x, what is the wedge, what is the expansion path? Impact: market size times social and economic ripple, tied explicitly to the Outcome layer of the value chain. The jury is three people — one public-sector expert, one private-sector expert, one Underdogs coach. We borrowed this rubric from the SU Team proposal because it is clean, it is balanced, and it explicitly refuses to separate business-model rigor from impact. Growth and Impact sit on the same rubric, with equal weight.

## Slide 38 — Pre-event: LMS/VOD Content Design
**Duration**: 3 min

**Speaking notes**:

> Pre-event is 2 to 3 weeks of async content. Five to eight videos, 10 to 15 minutes each — never longer. Three to five short readings, under 10 minutes each. One pre-event individual exercise, due before arrival. Video content design principle: each video answers exactly one question. "What is the problem track?" "What does a good BM look like in this track?" "Who are the 3 reference founders to know?" Never lecture. Never abstract. Every video ends with a specific artifact the viewer should produce. The individual exercise is a 1-page problem-framing sheet that the team will use on Day 1 block 3. Without the pre-event exercise, Day 1 block 3 collapses into 2 hours of confusion.

**Transition**: "Day 1, minute by minute."

**Duration**: 2 min

The non-obvious move is pre-assigning lunch seating on Day 1 — it accelerates team formation by about 90 minutes.

## Slide 40 — Day 2 Minute-by-Minute Timeline
**Duration**: 2 min

The next-steps briefing is not optional. Teams who leave without a clear "what happens Monday" answer do not convert to Stage 2.

## Slide 41 — Coaching Cue Cards
**Duration**: 3 min

**Speaking notes**:

> Coach cue cards distill 800 coaches' worth of experience into four rules. One — ask, do not tell. Start every session with "what is the question you are stuck on." Two — anchor on the value chain. If the team cannot name their Outcome, that is the problem, not their logo. Three — forbidden phrases. Do not say "that's a great idea." Do not say "I would do it differently." Both block the team's agency. Four — hand off explicitly. At the end of every session, "here is what I would work on in the next 2 hours" — a specific next move, not homework. What to avoid: turning coaching into consulting. A coach is not a co-founder. If a team cannot articulate the BM without you, they fail the assessment, and that is honest information.

**Transition**: "After the hackathon closes."
