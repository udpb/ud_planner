# 🇮🇳 India — Action AI 1N2D Launchpad

**Theme**: Build Many Entrepreneurs at Scale — Action AI 1-Night-2-Day Launchpad
**Program lines**: Fin-Act (financial-access & literacy builders) + AI Literacy (AI-application builders)
**Dominant 5D**: **Design (D4)** — MVP prototyping with AI tools
**Secondary 5D**: Direction (D2) — track-choice discipline
**Tagline**: "Not a course, a launchpad."
**Day 1 end-state**: every team has a **live, deployed product URL**.

---

## 0. Program Philosophy

### Scale many, not few

Action AI does not coach 10 unicorns. The funnel is **20,000 → 1,000 → 100 → 10 finalists**, and the 1N2D is the compression engine moving a university cohort from "I heard of AI" to "I shipped a product." Three facilitator decisions follow:

1. **No hand-holding past a point.** Not a coaching retreat. If a team cannot ship by Day 1 end, they do not advance — that is fine.
2. **One 5D per 1N2D.** You cannot install all 5D in 36 hours. India picks **Design (D4)** — testing assumptions with an MVP — because AI tools (Lovable especially) make Design the most teachable competency in compressed time. Direction (D2) supports so teams don't drift across tracks.
3. **Launchpad, not course.** Deliverable is a live URL, not a slide deck. Slides-only at Day 1 end = behind schedule.

### Stage integration

The 1N2D is **Stage 1.5** — an optional campus bootcamp on top of Action AI's Stage 1 (8h self-paced LMS), feeding Stage 2 (5–10 hrs/wk × 3 months).

```
Stage 1 (self-paced, 8h)  →  Stage 1.5 (1N2D campus, 36h)  →  Stage 2 (3-month batch)  →  Stage 3 (Korea Grand Showcase, top 10)
```

## B. Day 1 — From Problem to Live URL

### B1. 09:00–10:00 — Opening + Icebreaker
- 15 min: "Launchpad not course" keynote. 20,000 → 10 funnel slide. Korea Showcase on the wall.
- 20 min: Fin-Act vs AI Literacy line intros; lines sit on different sides.
- 25 min: Problem speed-dating — 5 rounds × 5 min; pitch the 3-line intake to a new partner each round.

### B2. 10:00–12:00 — Problem deep-dive + Track selection
- 30 min: Track menu walkthrough (Section 3).
- 60 min: Problem interviews in pairs (15 min each way). Coaches float with cue-card prompts.
- 30 min: **Prompt conversion** — convert interview notes into the first Gemini "problem prompt" of the day.

**Deliverable (B2 end)**: one-sentence problem statement + chosen track, on a sticky note on the track wall.

### B3. 13:00–15:00 — Team formation + Ideation
- 30 min: Track-wall clustering (adjacent problems cluster physically).
- 30 min: Team formation (3–5 people). Each team needs one member who can drive Lovable.
- 60 min: Divergent ideation with Gemini — 10+ directions, converge to 1.

**Deliverable**: one-paragraph product spec + sketched user flow.

### B4. 15:00–18:00 — MVP Sprint #1 (Lovable deploy)

The block the whole day points at. Every team must ship a deployed URL by 18:00.
- 15 min: Lovable crash intro — prompt → app → deploy.
- 135 min: Build. Coaches run 2 × 20-min 1:1s per team.
- 30 min: **Live URL parade** — every team opens their URL on a coach's phone. No URL = recovery watch for B5.

### B5. 19:00–22:00 — Evening sprint + social bonding
- 60 min: Dinner (informal coaching).
- 120 min: Sprint #1.5 — fix what broke in the parade; add one "wow" feature using Claude Code for tricky logic.

**Day 1 end-state check**: live, responsive, solves the stated problem at MVP level. Full checklist in Section 5.

## C. Day 2 — From Live URL to Demo Day

### C1. 08:00–10:00 — MVP Sprint #2 (polish)
Harden the MVP: empty-states, error handling, landing page. Coaches push off feature-creep onto demo narrative.

### C2. 10:00–12:00 — Dry-run + coach feedback
5-min rehearsal + 10-min feedback per team. Hammer the BM axis: "Who pays? How much? Via what channel?"

### C3. 13:00–15:00 — Demo Day (5-min pitch + 3-min Q&A)
External judges: 1 public-sector + 1 private-sector + 1 Underdogs coach. Every team demos the **live URL**, not slides-of-a-URL.

### C4. 15:00–16:00 — Judging + awards
Live university leaderboard (4-axis score). Top 3 announced. Stage 2 invites handed out.

### C5. 16:00–17:00 — Next-steps briefing
Stage 2 onboarding · Korea Grand Showcase criteria (Section 7) · Alumni Slack invite.

## 1. AI Tool Primer (one-page)

Teach this in B4 intro. Refer teams back to it all day.

| Tool | When to use | When NOT to use |
|---|---|---|
| **Gemini** | Divergent ideation · problem-interview synthesis · copy generation · market research summaries | Code generation (weaker than Claude Code), deployment |
| **Lovable** | Day 1 core tool. Prompt → web app → live URL in one flow. Handles front-end, back-end scaffold, and deploy. | Complex business logic that needs debugging. Switch to Claude Code for that. |
| **Claude Code** | Day 1 late + Day 2 morning. Tricky logic, API integrations, data transformations, bug fixing the Lovable output. | First-draft ideation (Gemini is faster for that). |

**Rule of thumb for coaches**: "Gemini to think, Lovable to ship, Claude Code to fix."

## 2. Fin-Act vs AI Literacy — Coaching Differentiation

Every team is tagged **[FIN-ACT]** or **[AI-LITERACY]** at registration. Coaches adapt their 1:1 prompts accordingly.

### Fin-Act prompts (financial-access, literacy, inclusion builders)

1. "Who is financially excluded by your current design, and what would it take to include them?"
2. "What's the smallest transaction you can handle profitably? The largest?"
3. "If a user can't read English or a smartphone keyboard, does your MVP still work?"
4. "What regulation (RBI / UPI / SEBI / local) would you bump into at 10,000 users?"
5. "Where does trust come from for your first 100 users — brand, community, or a human intermediary?"

### AI Literacy prompts (AI-application builders)

1. "Where is the AI earning its keep — core feature, or decoration?"
2. "What breaks if the AI is wrong 20% of the time? Does the user notice? Does it matter?"
3. "Could you replace the AI with a rule-based system for v0? Why or why not?"
4. "What's your prompt strategy — engineering prompts, or typing and hoping?"
5. "What data does the user give, and what does the AI give back? Is that exchange fair?"

## 3. Track Briefs — The 6 Action AI Tracks

Teams pick exactly one track at B2. Coaches enforce the pick — no drifting between tracks after B3.

### Track 1 — Access & Regional Divide
**Problem**: Rural, island, tier-3 populations cut off from banking, healthcare, gov services, market pricing.
**Example directions**: (a) voice WhatsApp agent for gov-scheme eligibility; (b) offline-first price-discovery for farmers; (c) vernacular chatbot for public-health.
**Pitfalls**: (1) Assuming smartphone + data = access. (2) Building in English when the user speaks Bhojpuri / Tamil / Marathi.

### Track 2 — AI Generation × Senior Life
**Problem**: India's aging cohort — medication, loneliness, intergenerational communication, pension access.
**Example directions**: (a) voice pension-claim assistant; (b) family-shared medication reminder with photo ID; (c) AI letter-writer bridging seniors with grandkids abroad.
**Pitfalls**: (1) Designing for the 25-year-old founder, not the 65-year-old user. (2) Forgetting the family member (not the senior) is often the first user.

### Track 3 — Industrial Transformation (manufacturing + agriculture)
**Problem**: MSMEs and smallholder farmers need AI-assisted operations — quality inspection, yield prediction, supply-chain — without CAPEX.
**Example directions**: (a) phone-camera defect detection for textile MSMEs; (b) crop-disease identifier with treatment; (c) WhatsApp-native inventory tracker for kirana-linked FMCG.
**Pitfalls**: (1) Demo works on clean photos, fails on a dusty factory floor. (2) Ignoring the analog workflow the buyer already trusts.

### Track 4 — Everyday Safety
**Problem**: Women's safety, road safety, food-adulteration, site safety — AI + mobile as a safety layer.
**Example directions**: (a) women-commuter companion with live trusted-contact status; (b) food-label adulterant scanner; (c) helmet-compliance camera for site supervisors.
**Pitfalls**: (1) False-positive panic worse than the original problem. (2) Privacy surveillance framed as "safety" — check Impact axis.

### Track 5 — Climate & Circular Economy
**Problem**: Waste segregation, water stress, hyper-local air quality, circular-economy marketplaces.
**Example directions**: (a) household-recyclables photo-classifier linked to kabadiwala pickup; (b) hyper-local AQI with activity recs; (c) secondhand resale with AI pricing.
**Pitfalls**: (1) Awareness app without behavior change — push for the action loop. (2) Greenwashing a pure marketplace.

### Track 6 — Social Cohesion & Open Track
**Problem**: Free choice — civic tech, language preservation, caste/class bridge-building, or anything not covered above.
**Example directions**: (a) vernacular tutor for minority-language speakers; (b) civic-issue reporter that routes to local authority; (c) anti-misinformation fact-check bot for family WhatsApp groups.
**Pitfalls**: (1) "Cohesion" is not a product — push for concrete user and moment. (2) Picking Open because the team couldn't decide — that's a D2 failure; coach should redirect.

## 5. Live-Deploy Checklist (Day 1 end, 22:00 cutoff)

A team passes B4/B5 if and only if **all six** are true:

- [ ] **Live**: URL loads on an unfamiliar phone without errors.
- [ ] **Responsive**: works on a 360px mobile viewport.
- [ ] **Solves the stated problem**: B2 problem statement addressed at MVP level — one core flow end-to-end.
- [ ] **AI visibly in use**: at least one real AI interaction, not mocked.
- [ ] **Self-onboarding**: no coach needed to explain what to click first.
- [ ] **Screenshot in team Slack**: evidence timestamped before cutoff.

2+ fails → **recovery watch** C1 morning. 4+ fails → no C3 demo; retrospective instead.

## 6. Assessment Rubric (4 axes × 25%)

| Axis | Weight | What judges look for | Fin-Act emphasis | AI Literacy emphasis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Idea specificity** | 25% | Concrete problem, non-obvious insight | Named user segment with income/geography | Named use-case where AI is load-bearing |
| **BM completeness** | 25% | Who pays, how much, via what channel | Unit economics at Rs.X per transaction | Free vs paid split; cost-per-AI-call |
| **Growth potential** | 25% | 10x wedge + expansion path | District → state → national rollout | Single-language → multi-language → vertical expansion |
| **Impact** | 25% | Market × social/economic ripple × **AI-appropriateness** | Number of financially-excluded users served | **Did AI actually add value, or was it performative?** |

### AI-appropriateness lens (embedded in Impact)

Judges score a sub-question: *If we removed the AI from this product, does it still work and is it still valuable?*

- **If yes → still valuable**: AI is decorative. Deduct Impact points.
- **If yes → still works but much worse**: AI is additive. Full points.
- **If no → it doesn't work at all without AI**: AI is core. Full points + judge note.

Jury: 3 people — 1 public-sector expert + 1 private-sector expert + 1 Underdogs coach.

## 8. Coach Cue Cards

### Core cue cards (B2, B4, C2)

1. **"Show me the user you drew this for. One person. Name, age, one sentence about their day."** (Specificity — D4 signal.)
2. **"Where is the AI earning its keep? Point to the exact screen."** (AI-appropriateness.)
3. **"If I gave you Rs. 10,000 tomorrow, what's the first thing you spend on growth?"** (Direction + BM.)
4. **"What did you try in the last 2 hours that didn't work?"** (Design iteration habit.)
5. **"Your demo story in one sentence — problem, hero move, outcome?"** (Pitch compression for C3.)

### India cultural-context cue cards (use sparingly)

1. **Hierarchical dynamics**: *"The senior member is doing most of the talking. Can the youngest member walk me through the MVP? I want their framing."* — surfaces junior ideas that default hierarchy suppresses.
2. **English-Hindi code-switching**: *"Pitch in the language your actual user speaks. If that's Hindi, pitch in Hindi — we won't mark down for language. We will mark down if it sounds like a judge-pitch, not a user-pitch."* — breaks the English-is-better reflex for vernacular users.

### Coach anti-patterns

- Do NOT solve the problem for them. Ask, don't answer.
- Do NOT allow track switches after B3 — that's a Direction failure.
- Do NOT grade on polish; grade on the live URL working.
- Do NOT tolerate slide-only demos at C3. The URL is the product.
